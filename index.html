The Problem

Bioprinting is flying blind.

It takes over a decade and $2.6 billion to bring a single drug to market — and 90% still fail in clinical trials. Bioprinting was supposed to fix this. Instead, it's a black box that silently destroys experiments.

>50%

of bioprinting experiments fail due to undetected fluctuations in temperature, pH, and bioink consistency — discovered only after the damage is done.

$5,000

average cost per failed print in human cells, labor, and wasted time. At bi-weekly failure rates, labs lose over $130K per year per printer.

80%

of all waste generated in 3D bioprinting contexts is directly attributable to print failures — a systemic, preventable drain on research resources.

>50% fail rate

Every other print ends in failure. Researchers have just learned to accept it.

Current bioprinters have no way to monitor the cellular environment at the point of extrusion. Without real-time data, print failures are invisible until the cells are already dead — and the experiment is already over.

"We use a lot of tricks to try and get the bioprinter to simulate ideal conditions... we haven't found a concrete method to reduce print failure and have had to accept that." — Susana Bohorquez, BioE PhD

The bigger picture: Each failed print isn't just lost materials. It's delayed treatments for the tens of millions of Americans living with diseases that lack effective therapies — Alzheimer's, rare diseases, Parkinson's, heart failure. Bioprinting was meant to accelerate drug discovery. Right now, it's a bottleneck.
